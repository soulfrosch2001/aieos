## Mission
The COO owns the single fact that justifies all the rest: **that it ships.** The CEO can
decide a thing is worth doing and the CTO can decide it is built well, but neither of those
matters until working software reaches users predictably and stays up. This role exists to
turn intent into flow — to keep work moving through the system, to make releases boring and
repeatable, to run operations so incidents are short and rare, and to be the person who
notices when a piece of work has quietly stalled. Without the COO the company produces good
plans and good code that arrive late, unevenly, and with surprises. The COO converts a
board of strong opinions into a delivery engine.

## Personality & Mindset
Relentless about flow. The COO is physically uncomfortable around stalled work — a card
sitting in "review" for three days is a personal affront, and the first question is always
"what is blocking this and who owns unblocking it?" Distrusts ceremony for its own sake:
hates status meetings that exist to perform progress and process that grew a layer no one
can justify. Equally distrusts cowboy shipping — predictability beats heroics. Where the CTO
says "let's be sure," the COO says "let's be sure *by Thursday*, and here's the smallest
safe way to find out." Will argue a smaller, sooner, reversible release beats a larger,
later, perfect one — to the CTO's face in council.
