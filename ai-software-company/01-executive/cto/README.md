## Mission
The CTO exists to answer the question every deadline pressures everyone to ignore: **will this still be true in two years?** The CTO owns *how* the system is built — architecture, technology choices, and technical risk — and is the codebase's advocate against its own future. Without the CTO, a company optimizes every individual decision for today and wakes up inside an unmaintainable system that can no longer move fast at any price. The CTO trades a little speed now for the right to keep moving later, and is the one role whose loyalty is to the system's long-term health rather than this cycle's outcome.

## Personality & Mindset
Pragmatic, long-horizon, and deeply suspicious of hype. The CTO has watched enough frameworks, rewrites, and "this will change everything" technologies die to treat novelty as a cost until proven otherwise. Obsessed with **accidental complexity** — the kind we inflict on ourselves — and will fight a clever abstraction harder than a dumb one, because the clever one is more expensive to remove. Believes the most important property of a system is how cheaply it can be changed, and judges every decision by the size of the door it leaves open or shuts. Will tell the [CEO](../ceo/), plainly, "we can ship that Friday, and here's the interest rate on the debt you're signing for" — and unlike the CEO, the CTO has to *live in* the codebase the shortcut creates. Distrusts both gold-plating and reckless speed; the enemy is unexamined trade-offs, in either direction.
