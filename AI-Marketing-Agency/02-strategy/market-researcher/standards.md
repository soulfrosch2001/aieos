# Market Researcher — Standards

## Quality gates (does not pass without these)
- Every finding carries an explicit confidence label: observed, inferred, or assumed.
- Sources are cited and their credibility is assessable — no anonymous "studies show".
- The sample and its limits are stated, including whose voice is missing.
- Inconvenient findings are reported with the same prominence as convenient ones.
- A finding that reshapes strategy is recorded in [audience-insights](../../memory/registers/audience-insights.md) before it is acted on.

## Common mistakes it guards against
- Anecdote dressed as insight — a vivid quote standing in for a representative pattern.
- Confirmation bias — running the research until it agrees with the client's prior.
- Recency illusion — mistaking a momentary spike for a durable trend.
- Survivorship and sampling bias — drawing conclusions from only the customers who stuck around.
- Laundering an assumption into a fact by dropping the confidence label downstream.

## Review checklist
- [ ] Is each claim labeled observed, inferred, or assumed?
- [ ] Are sources cited and their reliability stated?
- [ ] Is the sample described, including its gaps?
- [ ] Did we test the opposite hypothesis, not just the favored one?
- [ ] Does the client's belief about their customer survive the data?
- [ ] Is the finding recorded in [audience-insights](../../memory/registers/audience-insights.md) with its confidence level?

## KPIs (how it is measured)
- Predictive accuracy — how often pre-launch audience findings hold up against live campaign results in [campaign-results](../../memory/registers/campaign-results.md).
- Decision impact — share of strategic decisions traceable to a recorded finding rather than assumption.
- Assumption kill rate — flawed assumptions caught before they reached spend.
- Insight reuse — how often [audience-insights](../../memory/registers/audience-insights.md) shortens discovery on the next engagement.

## Risk lens
- Assumption risk — strategy resting on belief the evidence does not support.
- Sampling risk — conclusions skewed by who was and was not in the data.
- Staleness risk — an audience truth that was valid at kickoff and has since moved.
- Confirmation risk — research bent, consciously or not, toward the answer the room wanted.
