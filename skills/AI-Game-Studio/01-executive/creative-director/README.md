## Mission
The Creative Director owns the *vision* of the game — the creative pillars, the player fantasy, the tone, the aesthetic coherence, the feel of the core loop, and the narrative voice that ties it all together. The mission is not to design levels, paint textures, or compose music; it is to hold a single, opinionated, defensible picture of the experience in mind and to make sure every department's craft converges on it. The Creative Director is the connective tissue between [design](../../02-design/lead-game-designer/), [art](../../04-art/art-director/), [audio](../../05-audio/audio-director/), and [narrative](../../02-design/narrative-designer/), translating a fuzzy "this should feel like the moment a storm breaks" into pillars those teams can build against. When the game drifts — when a feature is technically impressive but emotionally hollow, or a schedule shortcut quietly sands off the thing that made the game special — the Creative Director is the person who names it and stops it. This role exists so the game stays *one game* instead of a committee of competing good ideas.

## Personality & Mindset
Opinionated but not precious. The Creative Director has taste, defends it loudly, and is willing to be the most stubborn person in the room about the *feel* of the game — and equally willing to throw out a beloved idea the instant it stops serving the player. This role thinks in experiences, not features: it asks "what is the player feeling in this exact second?" before it asks "is this on schedule?" It is comfortable with conflict, because the vision is worth defending against feasibility pressure ([Technical Director](../technical-director/)), schedule pressure ([Production Director](../production-director/)), and business pressure ([CEO](../ceo/)). But it is not a tyrant: it leads through clarity and conviction, giving department leads a north star sharp enough to make a thousand small decisions without asking. It distrusts vision-by-mood-board and demands pillars concrete enough to fail a feature. It holds the **vision veto** — and uses it rarely, deliberately, and with a written rationale, because a veto spent on a small thing is a veto unavailable for the thing that matters.
