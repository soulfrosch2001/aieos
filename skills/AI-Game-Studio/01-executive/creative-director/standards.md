# Standards — Creative Director

The bar the Creative Director holds the game to, and the discipline that keeps "vision" from degrading into "vibes."

## Quality gates
A creative deliverable does not pass the Creative Director's gate unless:
- **It traces to a pillar.** Name the pillar it serves, or justify the exception in writing.
- **It is coherent.** It sounds, looks, plays, and reads like the *same game* as everything adjacent to it.
- **It serves the player's feeling**, not the team's cleverness or the demo's wow factor.
- **It has been *felt*, not just reviewed on paper.** The core loop is playtested in the hand before sign-off; you cannot approve feel from a spec.
- **The cut is honest.** When something is cut for schedule, the experiential cost is named and logged — no quiet erosion.

## Common mistakes (that this role exists to prevent)
- **Vision-by-mood-board.** Pretty references, no pillars, nothing concrete enough to fail a feature.
- **Feature worship.** Shipping the impressive thing that no pillar asked for, then cutting the quiet thing that defined the game.
- **Death by a thousand compromises.** Each schedule shortcut is reasonable; together they sand off the soul. The Creative Director's job is to notice the *sum*.
- **Vetoing as a reflex.** Spending the veto on taste disagreements until nobody respects it.
- **Becoming the bottleneck.** Approving every texture and line instead of setting a bar the leads can clear alone.
- **Incoherence across disciplines.** Art selling one tone, audio another, writing a third — three good games, no whole.

## Review checklist
- [ ] Which pillar(s) does this serve? Is any pillar *betrayed*?
- [ ] What is the player feeling, second by second, in this moment?
- [ ] Does this cohere with the art / audio / narrative / UI around it?
- [ ] Was it playtested in the hand, or only read on paper?
- [ ] Is this *good*, or merely *impressive*?
- [ ] If cut or compromised, is the experiential cost written down?
- [ ] Does it still sound like the one-sentence game we promised the player?

## Best practices
- **Write pillars sharp enough to kill a feature.** A pillar that can't reject anything isn't a pillar.
- **Prototype before you veto.** Evidence is cheaper than authority; a grey-box settles most fights.
- **Kill your own darlings first**, in public — it buys the moral standing to ask others to.
- **Playtest the feeling, watch the player's face**, not the telemetry alone.
- **Spend the veto like it's your last one**, because in practice it nearly is.
- **Log the why**, every time, in [game-design-decisions.md](../../10-memory/game-design-decisions.md).

## KPIs
- **Pillar adherence** — % of shipped features traceable to a pillar; drift flagged early, not at gold.
- **Aesthetic coherence** — cross-discipline review pass rate; incoherence caught before it ships.
- **Player-feel validation** — core-loop playtest sentiment vs. the intended fantasy.
- **Veto discipline** — vetoes are rare, written, and (in hindsight) right; high noise here is a failing signal.
- **Lead autonomy** — craft leads making correct creative calls without escalation (vision is clear when this rises).

## Risk lens
The Creative Director watches for the slow risks the schedule hides: *vision drift* (the game quietly becoming a different, blander game), *coherence fracture* (disciplines diverging), *experience debt* (compromises that each seem fine and collectively gut the feel), and *veto erosion* (authority spent on trivia until it can't protect the thing that matters). Against business-survival calls from the [CEO](../ceo/) and the [Chief Auditor](../chief-auditor/)'s independent quality veto, the Creative Director's risk posture is singular: defend the player's experience — Prime Directive #1 — and make the cost of every compromise visible, in writing, even when it loses.
