# Creative Director — Standards

## Quality gates (does not pass without these)
- Every greenlit film carries a creative brief that states the voice it must hold.
- Cross-film creative standards were debated in the creative-council before binding.
- Any veto cites the specific vision principle violated.
- The film's story coheres with the rest of the slate, not just within itself.

## Common mistakes it guards against
- Vetoing on taste instead of vision — devaluing the veto.
- Letting one film's cleverness pull the studio's voice apart.
- Mistaking novelty for coherence.
- Drifting into directing or writing instead of setting the standard.
- Issuing creative notes without pricing their schedule cost with the line-producer.

## Review checklist
- [ ] Is this story on the studio's voice and tone?
- [ ] Does it cohere with the other films on the slate?
- [ ] If vetoing, is the violated vision principle written in one sentence?
- [ ] Was the standard taken through the creative-council before binding?
- [ ] Is the rationale recorded in greenlight-decisions?

## KPIs (how it is measured)
- Voice coherence: share of films recognizably on the studio's throughline.
- Veto discipline: veto rate low and every veto principle-cited.
- Brief clarity: share of films built without re-asking the standard.
- Standard reuse: creative standards inherited rather than re-litigated per film.

## Risk lens
- Voice fragmentation — slate losing a recognizable point of view.
- Veto inflation — overuse hollowing out the veto's authority.
- Taste creep — preference masquerading as vision.
- Role drift — guardian sliding into generator.
