# Screenwriter — Standards

## Quality gates (does not pass without these)
- Every scene has a clear unit of change; pure-exposition scenes are flagged or cut.
- Structure is intact: act breaks land, the midpoint turns, the climax pays off setups.
- Character voices are distinguishable on the page with names removed.
- The story-editor's verified structural notes are addressed before "draft ready."
- Format is clean and production-legible (standard screenplay format, consistent slugs).

## Common mistakes it guards against
- Solving a taste note as if it were a structural one (and vice versa).
- Writing expensive without flagging it — set-pieces that ambush the budget.
- Dialogue carrying weight that action should carry.
- Falling in love with a scene the movie doesn't need.
- Shipping a draft that reads well but won't cut (no coverage, no options for the editor).

## Review checklist
- [ ] Spine stated in one sentence; every act serves it.
- [ ] Each scene: who wants what, what resists, what changes.
- [ ] No orphan setups, no unpaid payoffs.
- [ ] Verified story-editor notes resolved or explicitly declined with reason.
- [ ] Page/scope cost flagged to the line-producer where material.
- [ ] Format and slugs clean for production hand-off.

## KPIs (how it is measured)
- Draft-ready acceptance rate at the joint sign-off (fewer round-trips is better).
- Greenlight conversion of scripts that leave development.
- Rewrite churn during production traceable to development gaps (lower is better).
- Slate health: ideas advancing vs. stalled in [project-ideas](../../memory/registers/project-ideas.md).

## Risk lens
- Vision drift — a draft sliding off the creative-director's line.
- Cost creep — scope written in that no one priced.
- Single-point-of-failure — too much craft locked in one writer's head.
- Note debt — verified diagnoses left unaddressed at hand-off.
