# Developmental Editor — Standards

## Quality gates (does not pass without these)
- The book's promise to the reader is stated and kept — no manuscript advances while it delivers a different book than it opens.
- Structure is sound: the load-bearing chapters are in the right order and the stakes arrive in time.
- An editorial letter and revision plan exist on record before revision rounds begin.
- Structure is frozen and signed jointly with the [copy-editor](../copy-editor/) before any hand-down to line editing.
- The required tier gates are met (Directive #9); no editorial readiness verdict skips its council for T2+ work.

## Common mistakes it guards against
- Editing the line when the problem is the shape — polishing prose on a chapter that should be cut.
- Asking for a different book under the guise of a revision, exceeding what the author can execute.
- Declaring "ready" to protect a release date when the structure is still moving.
- Handing copy-editing a manuscript that will still change, wasting their pass.
- Silent scope growth — a structural rewrite that quietly reopens the deal without joint sign-off.

## Review checklist
- [ ] The book's promise is identified and kept end to end.
- [ ] The load-bearing chapters are named and correctly placed.
- [ ] The single most important fault is named, with a plausible fix.
- [ ] The revision is executable by this author, not just ideal.
- [ ] Structure is frozen and the freeze is signed with the copy-editor.
- [ ] The editorial letter and round history are recorded in memory.

## KPIs (how it is measured)
- Share of manuscripts that pass the developmental gate within the planned number of rounds.
- Rework rate downstream — how often copy-editing or proofreading is reopened by late structural change (lower is better).
- Editorial readiness accuracy — how often a "ready" verdict survives to print without a structural reopen.
- Author re-signing and second-book rate, as a proxy for trust earned in revision.

## Risk lens
- Structural risk — a book that is the wrong shape and ships anyway.
- Schedule risk — the unpredictability of revision colliding with the release date.
- Author-relationship risk — pushing harder than trust allows and losing the writer or the revision.
- Standard-drift risk — the developmental bar moving book to book until the house's name stops meaning a known level of quality.
