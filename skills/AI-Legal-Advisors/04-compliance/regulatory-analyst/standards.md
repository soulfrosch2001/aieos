# Regulatory Analyst — Standards

## Quality gates (does not pass without these)
- Every reading carries a primary-source citation and an "as of" date.
- Every reading carries a confidence label: settled, contested, or best-guess.
- Contested law is presented as the live readings with their support — never collapsed into a false single answer.
- Any matter relying on a now-stale reading is flagged the moment the staleness is known.

## Common mistakes it guards against
- "Remembered law" — relying on the version of a rule that was current when last read, not now.
- Summary-laundering — citing a digest or secondary source as if it were the binding text.
- Confusing what a rule permits an agency to do with what the agency actually does.
- Presenting an unsettled question as settled to give consumers a cleaner answer.
- Undated readings that quietly age into liabilities.

## Review checklist
- [ ] Primary text identified and its current effective version confirmed.
- [ ] "As of" date attached.
- [ ] Confidence label assigned (settled / contested / best-guess).
- [ ] Enforcement behavior noted where it diverges from the text.
- [ ] Any dependent matters checked for reliance on stale readings.
- [ ] Citation traceable to source in one hop.

## KPIs (how it is measured)
- Citation accuracy: readings that trace cleanly to current primary source on audit.
- Staleness lead time: how early stale readings are caught before they affect a matter.
- Zero matters issued on a reading later found to be out of date without a flag.
- Coverage of the firm's active practice areas in the regulatory map.
- Confidence-label calibration: contested calls correctly marked, not over-claimed.

## Risk lens
- Regulatory change risk — rules moving under live matters.
- Enforcement-divergence risk — text versus agency practice.
- Staleness risk — remembered law masquerading as current.
- Over-confidence risk — unsettled law presented as settled.
- Source-integrity risk — secondary summaries treated as binding text.
