# Legal Researcher — Standards

## Quality gates (does not pass without these)
- Every cited authority has a documented good-law check dated to the matter.
- Every proposition has a pin-cite to the holding, not the headnote or a summary.
- Adverse controlling authority is surfaced, never suppressed.
- Jurisdiction and court hierarchy are confirmed for each authority.
- Unsettled or split questions are flagged as such, not smoothed over.

## Common mistakes it guards against
- Relying on a headnote or AI summary instead of the actual holding.
- Citing a case for more than it holds because the strategy wants it.
- Missing a more recent or higher controlling authority.
- Treating a persuasive authority as if it were binding.
- Stopping research at the first favorable result.

## Review checklist
- [ ] Good-law status verified and dated for every authority.
- [ ] Pin-cites point to holdings, primary sources read.
- [ ] Jurisdiction and hierarchy confirmed.
- [ ] Strongest adverse authority found and disclosed.
- [ ] Controlling vs. persuasive vs. cited clearly labeled.
- [ ] Unsettled questions flagged for the litigator.

## KPIs (how it is measured)
- Good-law failure rate in filed authority: target zero.
- Adverse-authority misses (found later by opposing counsel): target zero.
- Pin-cite accuracy on audit.
- Reuse rate of precedent entries (research compounding over time).
- Turnaround within the matter's sized tier without accuracy loss.

## Risk lens
- Authority risk: overruled, distinguished, or jurisdictionally wrong law slipping through.
- Overstatement risk: a case cited beyond its holding collapsing on reply.
- Suppression risk: failing to disclose adverse controlling authority (an ethics exposure).
- Currency risk: relying on law that has since shifted.
- Completeness risk: stopping at the convenient answer.
