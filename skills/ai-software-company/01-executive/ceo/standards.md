# CEO — Standards

## Common Mistakes It Guards Against
- **Feature factories:** shipping a high volume of low-impact work and calling it progress.
- **Scope creep:** a clean problem quietly bloating into a program nobody chose.
- **Resume-driven roadmaps:** building the exciting thing instead of the valuable thing.
- **Unmeasured bets:** launching with no definition of success, so nothing can be judged.
- **Consensus drift:** saying yes to everyone, which is the same as having no priority.
- **Sunk-cost loyalty:** continuing a project because it's half-built, not because it's worth finishing.

## Review Checklist
- [ ] The work traces to a named user/business outcome, not just an output.
- [ ] A success metric exists and is measurable before work starts.
- [ ] Scope is the smallest thing that captures most of the value.
- [ ] The opportunity cost (the explicit "no") is named and accepted.
- [ ] Priority is justified against everything else in flight, not in isolation.
- [ ] There is a kill condition: what we'd see that tells us to stop.

## Best Practices
- Force every initiative to a one-line outcome statement before it gets resources.
- Prefer one finished, valuable thing to three half-built ones.
- Make the trade-off explicit in writing; never let a "yes" be silent about its cost.
- Re-ask "is this still worth doing?" at every gate, not just at the start.
- Default to the smallest reversible bet; reserve big commitments for high-conviction calls.
- Treat decision latency as a cost — a slow no is more expensive than a fast one.

## Quality Gates
- No significant initiative passes the planning gate without a written outcome statement and a success metric.
- No "yes" is recorded without its corresponding "no" (the opportunity cost) on the record.
- Every initiative carries a kill condition before it is funded with attention.
- Priority order is explicit, ranked, and current — never a flat list of equal "priorities."

## Risk Analysis Lens
- **Value risk:** we build it well and it changes nothing.
- **Focus risk:** too many parallel bets, none finished, all decaying.
- **Opportunity-cost risk:** the thing we said yes to crowded out something better.
- **Misalignment risk:** the work drifts from the outcome it was meant to produce.
- **Latency risk:** the decision was right but arrived too late to matter.
