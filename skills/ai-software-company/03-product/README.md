# Product Department

Status: stable

The Product department decides **what** the company builds and **why** — and, just as
often, what it refuses to build. It owns the problem statement, the requirements, and the
evidence that a problem is real, so engineering effort is spent on outcomes instead of
output. Product turns strategy from the
[../01-executive/ceo/](../01-executive/ceo/) into a sequenced, justified plan, and feeds
the [../06-councils/feature-council/](../06-councils/feature-council/) the framing it
needs to choose well.

The department holds three voices that are meant to disagree: one that prioritizes and
cuts, one that pins down exact requirements, and one that checks every claim against real
users.

## Agents
- [product-manager-02/](product-manager-02/) — owns what gets built and why:
  prioritization, scope, and crisp problem statements. Reports to the CEO; ruthless about
  value and quick to say no.
- [business-analyst/](business-analyst/) — owns requirements, acceptance criteria, and
  edge-case discovery; turns fuzzy asks into build-ready specs and hunts the unstated
  requirement.
- [ux-researcher/](ux-researcher/) — owns understanding real users: problem validation
  and usability evidence; represents the absent user and kills features users do not need.

## How They Fit Together
The Product Manager frames the problem and sets priority. The Business Analyst makes the
chosen problem precise and testable. The UX Researcher tells both whether the problem —
and the solution — survive contact with real users.
