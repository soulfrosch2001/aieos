# Standards — Progression Designer

## Quality Gates
- Every curve ships with its graph and its time-to-next-unlock at early, mid, and late checkpoints. No curve goes live as an unexamined formula.
- No phase of the game is dead air: early, mid, and late each have a live unlock cadence.
- Any system defended only by "it increases playtime" fails the gate on sight ([Prime Directive #1](../../00-company/COMPANY.md)).
- Curves touching monetizable grind carry an explicit player-time-cost note for the [Economy Council](../../08-councils/economy-council/).

## Review Checklist
- [ ] No grind walls disguised as content — every "more hours" beat delivers more *game*.
- [ ] Unlock cadence keeps the early, mid, and late game all alive and pulling forward.
- [ ] No dead levels — every level-up grants capability, choice, or meaningful reward.
- [ ] Power growth tracks skill growth; the number reflects real competence gained.
- [ ] Gates teach or pace, never merely punish.
- [ ] Reward schedules create delight, not compulsion — no Skinner-box patterns.
- [ ] Endgame respects the maxed player's time and offers a real reason to return.
- [ ] Any dissent on this system is recorded, not buried ([Prime Directive #4](../../00-company/COMPANY.md)).

## Common Mistakes
- **Exponential curves that wall out players** — costs that balloon until progress stalls and the mid-game player simply quits.
- **Front-loaded unlocks** that spend the whole toy box early and leave an empty, contentless endgame.
- **Treadmills** — progression that asks for ever more time while delivering the same experience.
- **Mandatory grind** — gating real content behind hours of repetition the player would never choose.
- **Numbers without competence** — inflating power to *feel* like growth while the player learns nothing new.

## Balancing Guidelines
- **Curve shapes:** *Linear* gives steady, predictable pacing but can feel flat late. *Exponential* creates a sense of escalating stakes but walls out players if costs outrun rewards — use with great caution. *Logarithmic* front-loads the thrill and gently tapers, ideal for respecting later-game time. Most healthy games are hand-tuned hybrids.
- **Time-to-next-unlock pacing:** keep it short and visible early to hook, stretch it deliberately later — but never to a length that feels like a tax. Always know the current value at every phase.
- **Power creep:** budget it. Each tier should outshine the last enough to feel good, not so much it trivializes prior content.
- **The core tension:** the *early-game hook* (fast, generous, seductive) fights *late-game retention* (paced, scarce, lasting). Resolve it with cadence, not grind — fresh unlocks, not longer timers.

## KPIs / Metrics
- **Unlock cadence** — frequency of new content/abilities across the playtime arc; no flat stretches.
- **Level-up frequency curve** — how often level-ups land over time; watch for cliffs.
- **Churn-by-level / drop-off points** — where players quit; a spike at level N is a curve defect to investigate.
- **Time-to-mastery** — how long until the player feels fluent; too long is a wall, too short is shallow.
- **% reaching endgame** — the fraction who arrive at the long tail; the truest test of whether the curve held.

## Best Practices
- Design the endgame first so the early game can promise it honestly.
- Pace by what the player *gains*, never by how long you can detain them.
- Make the next reward legible — the player should always sense how close they are.
- When in doubt, cut a level rather than pad one. Respect the player's time as if it were your own.
